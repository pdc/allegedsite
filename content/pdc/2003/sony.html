Alleged Topic: Sony

Do not buy Samsung digital cameras

23 February 2004

Topics

  • sony
  • samsung
  • camera

#

I hate discovering I've spend serious money on something that turns out to be no good—such as my Samsung DigiMax V3 digital camera.

When buying my camera I did my research and decided on some 3-megapixel camera with optical zoom and reasonably compact size. I dithered for ages between two cameras, a Sony Cybershot DSC-P32 (or P52? I forget the exact model number) and the Samsung. Both has my minimum spec. Both had lenses by reputable optics companies. Both seemed pretty much of muchness. Sony has Memory Stick; Samsung has MultiMedia Card. Arbitrarily I chose the Samsung.

The Samsung is a fine camera as far as it goes. Sadly it does not go for more than a few weekends—long enough for me to go on holiday and return (see my Mull album). At this point the non-rechargeable battery they supply is exhausted. Unlike the Sony, the Samsung does not come with a charger. I wish I had thought to check for rechargeability before I bought the thing!

Running the camera on disposable batteries (alkali or otherwise) is prohibitively expensive, not to mention a crime against the environment. I bought a Uniross charger and rechargeables. These ‘high power’ batteries (1800 mAh) last a matter of minutes, long enough for a half-dozen shots at best (see my Springy album). Replacements for the lithium battery the camera came with look to cost about £14 if anyone sold them. Samsung sell a rechargeable battery for some large quantity of money, at which point the camera is markedly more expensive than the Sony model.

During lunch today I was walking along the high street when I came across some Japanese tourists photographing the cherry blossom against the old yellow stone of St Mary’s church. A beautiful colour study which I could not capture myself because my camera was back in the office in my bag with two exhausted batteries inside it.

Today for the first time this year it was light enough to imagine taking a photograph on the way home. I swapped the exhausted batteries for ones that had been in the charger for some days. No dice—the camera would not even switch on. Presumably rechargeable batteries have to be charged and used immediately or something similarly impossibly inconvenient.

Anyway, the upshot of this is I have spent in the order of £300 to acquire a camera-shaped object that is not actually useable as a camera, together with a determination never to buy Samsung cameras again. If Samsung has bundled a charger with their camera my user experience would have been fantastic—up until it ran out of juice, the camera was great fun to use and took good pictures. As it is, the marketing people who decided to take the charger out of the package have negated the good work of dozens (hundreds?) of engineers and designers.
