About character encodings

Modern text-processing software is described in terms of Unicode (or, equivalently, ISO 10646). That is, operations are described in terms of character data, where characters are the code points defined in the Unicode universal character set. Examples of Unicode code points include U+0041 A (latin capital letter A), U+00C1 Á (latin capital letter A with acute), U+2014 — (em dash)

These numbers must be encoded as sequences of bytes if they are to be stored in files on disc, or transmitted over networks, or passed as parameters to library functions. The simplest encodings use one byte to represent one character; this obviously limits them to at most 256 Unicode code points. Examples of single-byte encoding-schemes are ASCII, which encodes the first 128 code points of Unicode one-for-one (code 0x41 encodes U+0041, and so on), and cannot encode characters beyond U+007F, and ISO 8859-1 (also called Latin-1), which encodes the first 256 code points of Unicode.

Other single-byte encodings map bytes on to different Unicode characters—for example, ISO 8859-7 (Greek) maps characters in the range 0xC0 to 0xFF on to Greek letters (Unicode U+0370 onwards).

About English punctuation

In typeset books, quoted words and phrases may be distinguished from the rest of the text through a change of typeface (from upright to italic, for example), or through quotation marks. In English the marks of quotation are the apostrophe ’ (Unicode U+2019 RIGHT SINGLE QUOTATION MARK) and the inverted comma ‘ (Unicode U+2018 LEFT SINGLE QUOTATION MARK). In the days of hot-metal printing, the inverted (or turned) comma was literally a comma that was turned upside-down.

Note that Unicode uses the name APOSTROPHE for U+0027 ', a typewriter-inspired character that combines the roles of apostrophe, opening quotation mark, prime, and even acute accent. It is often called quote or tick and is important in writing in computer languages.

The glyphs for these characters vary according to the operating system your fonts were designed for. On many systems using the X11 window system, and many terminals, the glyphs for U+0027 and U+0060 look more appropriate for U+2018 and U+2019, reflecting the common convention of using the former as substitutes for the latter.
